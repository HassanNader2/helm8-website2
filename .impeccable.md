## Design Context

### Users

**Primary:** UAE SME business owners — trading companies, contracting firms, healthcare clinics, logistics operators. Typically 35–55, running a 10–150 person business. They manage back-office operations manually or with spreadsheets. They are skeptical of tech vendors and have been burned by software that promised automation but required a full-time operator to maintain.

**Secondary:** Operations managers and finance managers who report to the business owner and do the manual work themselves. They are the ones who will feel the direct pain relief most acutely.

**Context of use:** The owner lands on the site from a cold email or a referral. They are on a laptop, probably mid-working day. They are busy and distracted. They have seen ten other "automation" pitches this year. They give the site 8–12 seconds before deciding whether it's worth reading.

**Job to be done:** Determine in the first 3 seconds whether the site speaks to a problem they actually have — not whether Helm8 exists or what automation is. The decision trigger is recognition ("that's exactly what's happening in my business"), not education.

### Brand Personality

**Three words:** Precise, operational, earned.

- **Precise:** The work is exact. Invoice captured in 40 seconds, not "automatically." 52 days to collect payment reduced to 19, not "significant improvement." The design reflects this — nothing decorative that doesn't earn its place.
- **Operational:** This is back-office infrastructure, not consumer software. It should feel like the inside of a well-run firm, not a startup's marketing site.
- **Earned:** Every claim is backed by a number. Every number is from a real business. The design communicates credibility through restraint, not assertion.

**Emotional goal for first-time visitor:** "They get me." Not impressed, not dazzled — understood. The pain point framing should land before any product pitch. The visitor should feel like the site was written by someone who has sat in their operations manager's chair, not by a vendor trying to sell them something.

**Anti-reference:** Enterprise software vendor. No stock photography of diverse teams in glass offices. No "solutions" language. No badge walls or certification logos as credibility signals. No IBM-blue or corporate-gray palette. No copy that describes features instead of outcomes.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Visual tone:** Editorial precision meets operational seriousness. Closer to a well-designed financial report than a SaaS product landing page. Closer to a consulting firm's site than an agency's portfolio.

**Primary reference:** Stripe.com — B2B conversion discipline, restrained palette, outcome-first structure, never showy. Every section earns its presence.

**Execution quality reference:** Linear.app — care in every pixel, intentional whitespace, nothing templated.

**Copy tone reference:** Loom.com — direct, human, specific. Sounds like a person who understands the problem, not marketing copy.

**Theme:** Light mode primary, dark mode supported. The site is used during business hours, on a laptop, in a lit office. Light mode is the correct default for this audience and context.

**Typography:** Schibsted Grotesk — single font, multiple weights. Strong at 800 for display headlines, clean at 400 for body. Editorial character without startup vibes. Not in the reflex font reject list.

**Color palette:** Amber/cream existing palette — keep the amber as the sole accent. `primary-container #b8902a` is the visible gold accent. `surface #fafaf5` is the base. All surfaces tinted subtly toward amber. The 60/30/10 rule applies strictly — amber is rare, which is what makes it land.

**Motion:** Purposeful and minimal. Entrances only. No ambient decoration. `ease-out-quart` easing. Stagger only when it adds clarity (lists, stat grids). `prefers-reduced-motion` respected everywhere.

### Design Principles

1. **Pain before product.** Every section leads with a problem, not a feature. The visitor should recognize their situation before they hear about Helm8.

2. **Specificity over assertion.** "3 days recovered every month" beats "save time." Numbers from real case studies are the primary credibility signal — never generic claims.

3. **Restraint earns trust.** Skeptical B2B buyers distrust visual excess. Every decorative element must justify its presence. If it doesn't help the reader understand or decide, it does not belong.

4. **Rhythm over uniformity.** Sections should breathe differently from each other. Tight problem cards, generous case study spreads, sparse CTAs. Visual monotony signals that no one made actual design decisions.

5. **The amber accent is a signal, not a theme.** Use it once per section, at most. It marks what matters — a result, a key phrase, a conversion action. Overuse kills its function.
